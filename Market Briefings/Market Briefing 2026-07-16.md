### fed-path — US Fed path & inflation — TURNING (whipsaw, net dovish into today)
- **What changed (24h):** Hike odds for the 28–29 July FOMC spiked to ~46.5% mid-week on hawkish Fed talk (Gov. - **Market effect:** 10Y ticked up ~3bp to ~4.59% despite the retreat in hike odds; DXY firmed modestly (~100.6) after two straight down days.
- **Concept:** Fed funds futures translate every data print into a probability-weighted bet on the next meeting. This week the odds swung twice in three days — hawkish speak and oil pushed toward "hike," then consumer data pulled back to "hold." That whipsaw, not the odds themselves, is the signal: the market has no settled view of this Fed yet.

### hormuz-oil — Hormuz / oil shock — STRENGTHENING (escalation risk widening)
- **What changed (24h):** US forces ran a seven-hour strike operation against Iranian coastal military assets Wednesday. The Joint Maritime Information Center raised its Hormuz transit advisory to "severe," the highest tier; war-risk insurance has risen to 2–6% of a vessel's value (from a fraction of a percent pre-conflict) and visible shipping through the strait has largely stopped. More serious: reports that Washington is weighing a strike on Kharg Island, Iran's main oil export terminal, plus Iranian threats to close the Bab el-Mandeb strait via Houthi allies — a second chokepoint.
- **Market effect:** Brent rose a fourth straight session to ~$85.28. Goldman now frames a bimodal range — above $110 in Q4 if the Gulf export recovery stalls, or back into the $60s if tensions ease. Korea's central bank explicitly cited oil-driven inflation (CPI 3.2% y/y) as a reason to hike today — the first clear transmission of the oil shock into a G10-adjacent policy decision.
- **Concept:** The market has been pricing "probability of disruption to transit," which is why Brent has moved in single dollars despite active fighting. A strike on export infrastructure itself is a different order of risk — it targets the oil, not just the route past it — which is why this is a genuine escalation, not a repeat of last week's headlines.

### ai-capex — AI capex cycle — TURNING (bifurcating: logic/foundry strong, memory in crisis)
- **What changed (24h):** Two things happened the same day, pointing opposite ways. TSMC — the AI-demand "referee" — posted record profit, raised 2026 capex guidance to $60–64bn (from $52–56bn) and revenue growth guidance to "slightly more than 40%," and pledged an extra $100bn for Arizona (total $265bn) — an unambiguous "demand is not slowing" signal. Meanwhile Korea's memory complex fell apart: Kospi -6.37%, Samsung -8.77%, SK Hynix -11.53%, reversing Wednesday's +6.24% rally outright. Separately, China's DRAM champion [[CXMT]] opened IPO subscriptions on Shanghai's STAR Market, targeting ~$8.6bn at an ~$85bn valuation ahead of a 27 July listing — a concrete data point behind the "Chinese memory oversupply" fear driving Korea's selloff.
- **Market effect:** TAIEX ended flat (TSMC clawed back an intraday 600-point loss before earnings landed); Nikkei -2.79%; Hang Seng +2.13%, bucking the region; US Nasdaq opened down (~-0.75%) on TSMC's cautious pricing commentary despite the beat.
- **Concept:** The capex debate is no longer "is the spending real" — TSMC just answered that with the cycle's biggest capex raise. It's splitting into "who captures the value." Foundry (TSMC) gets paid regardless of which chip design wins. Memory (Samsung, SK Hynix) is a commodity business where a well-funded entrant (CXMT) or a customer diversifying suppliers (Apple's reported China chip talks) directly threatens pricing — why Korea, not Taiwan, is today's epicenter.

### bond-regime — Bond market regime — STABLE (long end still backing up)
- **What changed (24h):** 10Y near ~4.59%, close to the two-month high of 4.62% touched July 13; 2Y roughly flat near 4.17–4.20%; 30Y sticky around 5.08%. Notably, yields rose even as the market pulled *forward-meeting* hike odds back down — growth data (Philly Fed) and oil-driven inflation risk are doing more work on yields than the next FOMC decision is.
- **Market effect:** Gold fell to roughly $4,000–4,040 (from ~$4,063), a genuine inflection since gold has held above $4,000 through weeks of war headlines. VIX rose to ~16.4 (+4.7%) — a real but modest pickup in risk-off, not panic. USD/SGD eased slightly to ~1.2884.
- **Concept:** When yields rise even as near-term policy odds fall, it means the [[Term Premium]] — the extra compensation investors demand for holding long-dated debt through an uncertain inflation and growth outlook — is doing the driving, not the fed funds path. Gold falling despite a live war is the tell: gold tracks *real* (inflation-adjusted) yields, and if nominal yields rise faster than inflation expectations, gold loses even when headline risk says it should rally.

## 5. Deep Dive — [[Bank of Korea]] Hikes Into a Crashing Market

**What happened:** The Bank of Korea raised its policy rate 25bp to 2.75% — its first hike since January 2023, a unanimous call matching every economist Bloomberg surveyed. Rationale: June CPI at 3.2% y/y (fastest since December 2023, driven partly by oil from the US–Iran conflict) and a won pinned above the psychologically important 1,500/USD level for over a month. Governor Shin Hyun-song, in office since April, has pushed for decisive inflation action. The bank projected 2.6% 2026 GDP growth, citing the AI-driven semiconductor export boom — the same boom whose equity expression, Kospi, fell 6.37% that same day. ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-16/bok-delivers-first-rate-hike-since-early-2023-amid-chip-led-boom), [Korea Herald](https://www.koreaherald.com/article/10810697))

**Why it matters:** A genuine regime turn, not a data blip — the first tightening by a significant Asian central bank this cycle, delivered on a day its domestic equity market was in freefall, which central banks usually hesitate to do. BOK's framing draws a hard line between the *equity* story (AI-capex doubt) and the *real-economy/export* story (semiconductor exports still booming) — inflation and the won justified the hike regardless of what Kospi did that morning.

**What's priced:** The hike itself was fully priced (unanimous consensus). What's *not* priced: whether this is the first of a hiking cycle or a one-off insurance move, and whether a stronger won from higher rates helps or hurts Korean exporters already under margin pressure from the memory rout.

**Monitor:** BOK's next meeting and any explicit commentary connecting the equity selloff to policy; won stability above/below 1,500/USD; whether the Fed's own hike-odds whipsaw this week converges or diverges from BOK's path, since a widening rate gap was itself cited as a reason to act now.

## 6. Idea Lab — [[Hanmi Semiconductor]] (042700.KS)

*Learning exercise, not a recommendation. Chosen as the "picks and shovels" name sitting directly between today's two AI-capex signals — TSMC's capex raise and Korea's memory-stock crash.*

**How it makes money:** Hanmi makes TC (thermal-compression) bonders — equipment that stacks and bonds the DRAM dies inside [[HBM]] (high-bandwidth memory, the specialized memory bolted onto every AI accelerator). It holds an estimated 71% global share of the HBM TC-bonder market, and in June won a ₩44.2bn order to supply HBM4 bonders to SK Hynix. It's now extending into 2.5D AI packaging equipment ("FC Bonder 3.5") to broaden beyond pure HBM bonding. Three KPIs matter: (1) the HBM capex plans of its real customers — Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron — since Hanmi's revenue sits one derivative removed from theirs; (2) new order/backlog announcements like the SK Hynix contract, its cleanest forward indicator; (3) diversification away from HBM-only bonding, since a two-customer book is a real concentration risk.

- **Bull in one line:** Hanmi is the toll booth on HBM capacity expansion — whichever memory maker wins the AI race, Hanmi collects on every new line built, and a ~71% share is hard to displace.
- **Bear in one line:** It's leveraged the wrong way too — if Meta's plan to resell excess AI compute, a CXMT-driven supply glut, or a genuine capex pause forces Samsung/SK Hynix to trim 2027 HBM plans, Hanmi sits one step removed from exactly the crash that hit Kospi today.
- **Differentiating question:** Is that ₩44.2bn HBM4 order — and Hanmi's roughly 180% year-to-date run — built on firm, multi-year capacity commitments from SK Hynix, or renewable annual contracts that could unwind as fast as today's Kospi move if 2027 HBM capex plans get trimmed?

## 7. Trade Floor — Two Open Tensions

**Equity — Is Kospi's whipsaw capitulation, or a new volatility regime?** One side: a +6.24% rally followed a day later by a -6.37% crash and another circuit breaker looks like a market flailing toward a bottom — violent two-way moves are a classic capitulation signature, and TSMC's same-week beat-and-raise contradicts the "demand is slowing" thesis driving the selloff. Other side: the whipsaw itself may *be* the information — this much volatility in Samsung and SK Hynix, ~60% of Kospi's cap, suggests genuinely lost confidence in near-term memory earnings, and TSMC's results don't help a memory maker about to face a state-backed Chinese competitor. *Settling datapoint: whether Samsung/SK Hynix stabilize into next week (Korea is shut Friday), and how HBM allocation language in upcoming hyperscaler earnings compares to Meta's "resell excess compute" framing.*

**FICC — Is BOK's hike ahead of a real inflation shift, or into a currency panic it will need to reverse?** One side: 3.2% CPI, a won pinned above 1,500, and oil in a genuinely bimodal $60–$110 range (Goldman) argue the hike is overdue — Korea is simply first to act on a real oil shock. Other side: US inflation has been cooling this week and Fed hike odds fell back to ~13%, so BOK may be tightening into a transitory, geopolitics-driven spike — the kind of move central banks have regretted before. *Settling datapoint: Korea's next CPI print, and whether Brent decisively breaks above $90 or reverts below $80.*
