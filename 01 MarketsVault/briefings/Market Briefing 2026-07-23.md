## 2. Theme Tracker

### 2.1 fed-path — US Fed path & inflation — stable (blackout holding, FOMC 5 days out)
- **What changed:** No new Fed commentary — the blackout period (18-30 July) continues to hold ahead of the 28-29 July FOMC meeting, Chair Warsh's first. Axios flagged that Warsh has effectively "ceded" his usual ability to shape expectations through silence, raising the odds of a volatile reaction to whatever the Fed says next week.
- **Market effect:** US 10-year Treasury held near ~4.66%, a fresh multi-month high, still being driven by the oil-linked inflation-risk channel (2.2) rather than any Fed signal.
- **Concept:** A blackout removes the Fed's ability to "talk the market down" between meetings. That's normally a minor detail — but with oil at $100 and a brand-new Fed chair about to deliver his first decision, the market has nothing but price action to read until 29 July, which is exactly why yields are moving on oil headlines rather than economic data this week.

### 2.2 hormuz-oil — Hormuz / Iran war / Red Sea — STRENGTHENING sharply (threat became reality)
- **What changed:** The single biggest development of the last 24 hours: Houthis fired on two Saudi-linked tankers, the *Encelia* (fire confirmed by Saudi state media) and the *Layla* (claimed, unconfirmed) — the first executed Red Sea attack since the 20 July blockade declaration, ending the "threat vs. reality" gap flagged in yesterday's briefing. Meanwhile the US struck Iran for a 12th consecutive night (a B-1 bomber was used for the first time this campaign), and Iran retaliated against US positions in Kuwait and Jordan. Trump threatened to destroy an Iranian bridge or power plant for every future tanker hit; Iran threatened regional energy infrastructure in response. The House approved $95bn in further Middle East war funding. Pakistan/Qatar floated a mediation proposal for both sides to revert to pre-9 July positions, but talks remain stalled. War-risk insurance: Red Sea premiums rose to >1% of hull value (from 0.3% a week ago); Hormuz premiums now 7.5-10% of hull value (from ~0.25% pre-war) — a $100m tanker now costs $3-10m to insure per transit.
- **Market effect:** Brent broke above $100/bbl for the first time since late May (from ~$94.07 Wednesday to ~$100.7), WTI ~$91; oil is up over 30% month-to-date. Notably, gold fell ~1.9% to ~$4,075-4,087 the same day — a cross-asset divergence worth watching (→ Trade Floor, 7.2).
- **Concept:** Markets price probability-weighted outcomes, and an *executed* strike carries far more information than a *declared* threat, because it resolves the market's biggest uncertainty — whether the Houthis were bluffing. That's why oil jumped 6-7% on two tanker strikes rather than grinding higher gradually: the market had been discounting some chance the blockade was rhetoric, and that discount just got priced out in one move.

### 2.3 ai-capex — AI capex cycle — TURNING further, now bifurcating between spenders and suppliers
- **What changed:** Wednesday's US session delivered the sharpest capex-discipline verdict yet: Alphabet fell ~7.1% (~$300bn) after raising 2026 capex guidance to $195-205bn from $180-190bn (previously reported as "held flat" — that framing understated it; the guide was actually raised again), with free-cash-flow margin compression the market punished. Tesla fell ~14.5% (~$200bn) on an EPS miss ($0.33 vs. ~$0.44 expected) tied to Cybercab/Optimus capex. Separately, IBM's Q2 call (Deep Dive, 5) confirmed enterprise IT budgets are being crowded out by the same memory-chip shortage. Yet the *suppliers* of AI infrastructure had the opposite day: Intel jumped >8% after-hours on a clean beat (Data Center & AI revenue +59%, 18A into high-volume manufacturing); Samsung (+3.65%) and SK Hynix (~+4%) led Kospi's 4.40% surge back above 7,000 as "chip peak-out" fears eased; TSMC recovered intraday and carries fresh price-target raises from Citi (NT$3,800) and UBS (NT$3,400, with UBS lifting 2026-28 capex forecasts).
- **Market effect:** Nasdaq -2.15% Wednesday on the Alphabet/Tesla reaction; Kospi +4.40%, Nikkei +0.46%, Intel +8%+ on the supplier side — a genuine same-week bifurcation, not a uniform "AI trade" move.
- **Concept:** The market is starting to separate AI capex into two distinct trades: the *spenders* (hyperscalers and enterprises funding the buildout, judged on free cash flow and ROI) and the *suppliers* (memory, foundry, equipment makers, judged on order books and pricing power). A dollar of capex is simultaneously bad news for the spender's near-term cash flow and good news for the supplier's revenue — which is exactly why Alphabet and Samsung can move in opposite directions on the same underlying capex dollar.

### 2.4 bond-regime — Bond market regime — turning, and now a global signal not just a UK one
- **What changed:** The ECB held its deposit rate at 2.25% as expected, but Lagarde's press conference genuinely shifted the narrative — she flagged inflation staying "well above target into the first half of 2027" on the energy shock and left the door open to a September hike, a reversal from the market's prior expectation of eventual cuts. UK gilts held near recent highs (10Y ~5.04-5.05%, 30Y ~5.75%) on the unresolved Healey/Burnham fiscal tension already in the tracker; sterling ~$1.337-1.338. US 10-year Treasuries held ~4.66% (→2.1).
- **Market effect:** European rate-cut pricing for September gave way to hike-risk pricing on the ECB's tone; UK and US yields both remain near multi-month highs; DXY roughly flat at ~101.1-101.2 as the moves offset across currencies.
- **Concept:** For over a year the global bond story was "when do central banks cut." An oil-driven inflation shock can flip that overnight, because central banks respond to the *inflation path*, not the *cause* of it — a war-driven $100 oil price feeds into the same CPI basket as demand-driven inflation would, which is why the ECB is now talking about a hike just months after markets expected the opposite.

## 5. Deep Dive — [[IBM]]'s Historic Crash and the AI-Capex Enterprise Crowd-Out Effect

**5.1 What happened:** IBM stock fell 25.2% on 14 July — its worst single trading day in over 110 years as a public company, wiping out roughly $68.8bn of market value — after management warned that enterprise clients are redirecting capital spending away from IBM's software and infrastructure services toward hardware: servers, storage and memory chips, made scarce and expensive by an escalating memory-chip shortage. The formal Q2 2026 earnings call landed 22 July: EPS of $2.93 and revenue of $17.16bn, both below estimates, with management trimming forward guidance. ([Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/petercohan/2026/07/15/ibm-stock-loses-67-billion--causes-and-recovery-outlook/), [Seeking Alpha transcript](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4924447-international-business-machines-corporation-ibm-q2-2026-earnings-call-transcript), [GuruFocus](https://www.gurufocus.com/news/8973425/international-business-machines-corp-ibm-q2-2026-earnings-call-highlights-navigating-revenue-challenges-and-strategic-investments))

**5.2 Why it matters:** This is a distinct mechanism inside the broader AI-capex story that the Alphabet/Tesla headlines don't capture. It isn't hyperscaler capex discipline — it's capex *crowding-out*: the same memory shortage enriching Samsung and SK Hynix (Theme Tracker, 2.3) is squeezing enterprise IT budgets elsewhere, as CIOs prioritize scarce, expensive hardware over software and consulting contracts. It suggests AI infrastructure demand has grown large enough to distort capital-allocation decisions well beyond the companies actually building AI models.

**5.3 What's priced in:** The 25% single-day drop already re-rated IBM's software/consulting franchise on the assumption that hardware-budget crowding-out persists for a while; the Q2 print — a genuine EPS and revenue miss, plus trimmed guidance — confirmed rather than reversed that repricing, which is why the stock's reaction to the formal results was comparatively muted next to the initial 14 July shock. What isn't yet priced: whether the memory shortage is a temporary supply-chain squeeze that eases within a few quarters, or a structural reallocation of enterprise IT spend toward AI-adjacent hardware that persists for years.

**5.4 Monitor:** Whether the same crowding-out dynamic shows up in other enterprise-software bellwethers' upcoming quarters (Microsoft, SAP, Salesforce, Oracle); DRAM/NAND spot-pricing trends as a leading indicator of when the shortage eases; and IBM's own guidance cadence over the next two quarters for signs of stabilization versus further deterioration.

## 6. Idea Lab — [[Nestlé]] (SIX: NESN / OTC: NSRGY)

*Learning exercise, not a recommendation. Rotation into European consumer staples — a deliberate change of pace from the week's chip/AI-capex names, and tied directly to today's earnings.*

**6.1 How it makes money:** Nestlé is the world's largest packaged-food company, built around Purina PetCare (its largest and highest-growth single category — dog and cat food, roughly a fifth of group sales), Nescafé/Nespresso coffee, Nutrition & Health Science (infant formula, medical nutrition, supplements) and confectionery/water, sold in around 190 countries. It reports by geographic zone (Americas; Europe, Middle East & North Africa; Asia, Oceania & Africa) plus globally-managed businesses. H1 2026 (reported 23 July): organic growth of 3.6%, up from 2.9% a year earlier — but the more important number is Real Internal Growth (RIG), the volume/mix component that strips out price increases, which jumped to 1.5% from just 0.2%, meaning more of this quarter's growth came from people buying more, not just paying more. Net profit, however, fell 31.4% to CHF 3.5bn from CHF 5.1bn, and the stock fell 6-8% on the print. Three KPIs matter most: (1) the RIG-versus-pricing split — the cleanest read on whether CEO Philipp Navratil's turnaround (he took over after predecessor Laurent Freixe's ouster in September 2025) is restoring genuine volume growth or is still price-dependent; (2) Purina PetCare's growth rate specifically, since it is both the largest and highest-margin category and effectively subsidizes weaker segments; (3) underlying trading operating profit (UTOP) margin — the gap between accelerating sales and shrinking net profit is the entire story this quarter. ([FoodNavigator](https://www.foodnavigator.com/Article/2026/07/23/nestle-h1-2026-organic-growth-up-but-net-profit-falls-314/), [Manila Times/GlobeNewswire](https://www.manilatimes.net/2026/07/23/tmt-newswire/globenewswire/half-year-results-2026-execution-driving-rig/2389935))

> [!bullish]
> **6.2 Bull:** RIG accelerating to 1.5% from 0.2% is the first real evidence the turnaround is restoring genuine volume growth rather than just raising prices on a shrinking customer base, and a stock reset lower on the profit miss offers a cheaper entry into a franchise that's genuinely improving underneath the headline number.

> [!bearish]
> **6.3 Bear:** A 31% net-profit decline against accelerating sales means costs — input, restructuring, one-offs — are eating every gain in volume, and one quarter of RIG improvement after years of price-led growth could just as easily be a temporary promotional push as a durable demand shift.

**6.4 Differentiating question:** Does Nestlé's Q3 update show RIG improvement broadening beyond Purina PetCare into its weaker categories — confectionery, water, developed-market nutrition — or does the volume gain stay concentrated in the one segment that was already strong? That tells you whether this is a company-wide turnaround or a single-category bright spot dressed up as one.

Or is this just Wednesday's panic and Thursday's relief showing up in two different corners of the same trade?** One side: Intel's 59% Data Center & AI revenue growth and 18A ramp, plus Samsung/SK Hynix's "peak-out fears easing" rally, are concrete revenue proof points, not promises. Other side: memory and equipment names are early-cycle and sell *into* hyperscaler capex — if that capex actually gets reined in down the line, the suppliers' current strength reverses just as fast as it appeared. *Settling datapoint: how Microsoft, Meta and Amazon's own capex guidance is received when they report in the coming weeks — a uniformly negative reaction across all hyperscalers would suggest the market is punishing capex itself, not just distinguishing spenders from suppliers.*

**7.2 FICC/commodity — Brent broke above $100 for the first time since late May on confirmed Houthi strikes on two Saudi tankers, yet gold fell about 1.9% the same day. Is the market treating this purely as a supply/inflation shock rather than a systemic risk-off event, or is gold just digesting a big prior run and due to catch up on the next escalation?** One side: with the ECB flagging a possible September hike and the Fed still in blackout, higher-for-longer rate expectations are a real headwind to a non-yielding asset like gold regardless of the war. Other side: gold remains up sharply for the year, and one session's pullback proves little — a confirmed strike inside the Strait of Hormuz itself, rather than the Red Sea, would likely reverse the divergence within hours. *Settling datapoint: whether gold reclaims $4,100+ if oil keeps climbing past $100, or the two keep decoupling — that tells you whether this episode is genuinely being priced as an inflation shock rather than a flight-to-safety one.*

## 11. Journal Seed

The most interesting thing that happened today wasn't the oil spike or the ECB — it was that Alphabet and Tesla got punished for AI capex on the same day Intel, Samsung and SK Hynix got rewarded for it. Same underlying capex dollar, opposite stock reactions, depending on which side of the transaction you sit on.

Is the market now consistently pricing AI infrastructure spend as good news for suppliers and bad news for spenders — a durable bifurcation you could actually trade a view on — or did Wednesday's panic and Thursday's relief just land in two different corners of the world by coincidence? Microsoft, Meta and Amazon's capex guidance in the coming weeks is the next real test.
