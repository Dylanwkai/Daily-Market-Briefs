## 2. Theme Tracker

### 2.1 fed-path — US Fed path & inflation — stable (odds reverted after a false alarm)
- **What changed:** CME FedWatch-style pricing swung back to ~87% odds of a hold at the 29 July FOMC meeting, down from the ~46% hike-odds figure reported Sunday — that figure looks to have been noise from a thin, single-source read rather than a genuine repricing. The underlying picture hasn't shifted: traders still price meaningful odds of a hike by September/October and treat December as near-certain, given resurgent oil-driven inflation risk. No new remarks from Chair Warsh have surfaced since Friday's testimony — the blackout period (18–30 July) is holding.
- **Market effect:** US 10-year Treasury essentially unchanged, oscillating 4.54–4.61% since Friday's 4.55% close. US equity futures modestly higher pre-market (S&P +0.1–0.3%, Nasdaq 100 +0.4–0.7%) as chips attempt a bounce, capped by oil/dollar strength.
- **Concept:** Thinly-traded prediction-market or futures-implied odds can swing 30+ percentage points in a day or two on a single data provider's read, then largely revert once more sources weigh in. The lesson isn't "the Fed changed its mind twice in 48 hours" — it's that any single snapshot of probabilistic pricing deserves corroboration before you treat it as a genuine market view, especially around illiquid or off-exchange contracts.

### 2.2 hormuz-oil — Hormuz / Iran war — STRENGTHENING sharply (from shipping-risk story to physical supply shock)
- **What changed:** The US struck Iran for a ninth consecutive night, hitting bridges, electrical infrastructure and a suspected underground missile-base city; total US service-member deaths in the conflict reached 18 (two in Jordan defending against Iranian missile/drone attacks, one in northern Iraq during unexploded-ordnance disposal). Iran declared its ceasefire with the US "effectively collapsed" and struck Kuwait's Al-Adiri camp and Ali Al Salem air base, plus a Kuwaiti desalination/power plant that knocked generation units offline — Kuwait sources roughly 90% of its drinking water from desalination. Iranian ballistic-missile shrapnel (aimed at Jordan) landed near Eilat, Israel; Israeli officials warned of an imminent "harsh counterstrike." Confirmed Hormuz crude/condensate transits collapsed to 5.1m bpd from 12.5m bpd a week ago — only 4 vessels transited Sunday versus 8 the day before and 100+ pre-conflict. Japanese refiners have asked Tokyo to release strategic stockpiles (Japan sources ~95% of its crude from Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, the UAE and Qatar, ~70% of it via Hormuz).
- **Market effect:** Brent broke above $90 Monday (intraday high ~$91.41), up 2–3% on the day and extending last week's 14–16% gain — the sharpest weekly move since April. WTI ~$82–83. US gasoline back above $4/gallon. War-risk hull insurance is up over 1,000% since late February, now roughly 3% of vessel value per voyage (~$7.5m per transit for a $300m VLCC); some insurers are cancelling Gulf war-risk cover outright rather than repricing it.
- **Concept:** There's a real difference between a *threatened* disruption, which shows up first in insurance premiums and options pricing while physical cargo still moves, and a *realized* one, where actual barrels stop flowing. This week crossed that line: transit counts near zero, not just costlier. War-risk premiums are also functioning as a shadow tax on trade — at $7.5m per transit, only the highest-value cargoes can still justify the trip, which is itself a form of supply rationing before any tanker is even hit.

### 2.3 ai-capex — AI capex cycle — TURNING, and now genuinely bifurcated (memory vs. compute)
- **What changed:** Korea's "Black Monday" extended Thursday's rout — Kospi -4.46% to 6,516.27, sell-sidecar circuit breakers triggered, Samsung and SK Hynix both down over 4%, Citi downgrading the index outright even as Barclays stayed bullish on semis broadly. But Bloomberg's Monday reframing of Kimi K3 as "more about memory than compute" cuts the other way for HBM/DRAM demand, and Taiwan's reaction was far milder — Taiex -0.5%, TSMC actually +1.31% after Friday's dip, having beaten estimates and raised 2026 guidance. Moonshot itself paused new Kimi K3 subscriptions after demand overwhelmed its own GPU capacity within 48 hours of launch — hard to square with a thesis that this model proves compute is now abundant.
- **Market effect:** Kospi -4.46% to 6,516.27; Nikkei stale at Friday's 64,141.12 (Tokyo cash market closed Monday for Marine Day, futures indicated weak); Taiex -0.5% with TSMC +1.31%; Hang Seng +1.11% and Shanghai +0.85% on separate China policy-support news (see 1.5).
- **Concept:** "Cheap compute displaces expensive compute" is the bear case — it hits demand for GPUs and logic chips directly. "Cheap compute still needs a lot of memory" is a different, more bullish case for HBM/DRAM suppliers, since even an efficient model needs somewhere to hold its parameters and context. Kimi K3's memory-hungry architecture is being read as evidence for the second story, which is why Korea's memory-heavy index cratered on reflexive fear while Taiwan's foundry-heavy index barely moved — and why a "sell sidecar" (a programmatic circuit-breaker that auto-triggers a wave of index-arbitrage sell orders once futures and cash prices gap far enough apart) can make a move look far more fundamentals-driven in the moment than it turns out to be.

### 2.4 bond-regime — Bond market regime — flat, but masking two large offsetting forces
- **What changed:** The 10-year Treasury is essentially unchanged from Friday, oscillating 4.54–4.61%, as a safe-haven bid from the Iran war and the Fed's odds-reversion toward "hold" (both yield-suppressing) offset fresh oil-driven inflation-fear (yield-raising). The DXY itself is roughly flat near 100.8, but broader dollar gauges (Bloomberg's own dollar spot index) jumped as much as 0.8% to their highest since early February on safe-haven and oil-linked flows — USD/JPY hit 162.48, its strongest since 9 July.
- **Market effect:** Gold holding just above $4,000 (~$4,017), roughly flat on the day. UK gilts are the exception — they sold off sharply on the Burnham cabinet reshuffle (see 1.3/Deep Dive), a country-specific move rather than a global bond-regime shift.
- **Concept:** An "unchanged" yield print isn't proof that nothing is happening — it can just as easily mean two large forces are cancelling out. Here, a war-driven flight-to-quality bid and a dovish Fed-odds correction are both pulling yields down at the same time oil-driven inflation fear is pulling them up. Always check whether a flat number reflects genuine calm or a tug-of-war before reading it as "no news."

## 6. Idea Lab — [[Zurn Elkay Water Solutions]] (NYSE: ZWS)

*Learning exercise, not a recommendation. Chosen for fresh M&A news today and as a rotation into industrials/small-mid-cap, away from recent editions' mega-cap tech and Asia-heavy picks.*

**6.1 How it makes money:** Zurn Elkay describes itself as a "pure-play water management business" — water safety and control products, flow systems, hygienic and environmental fixtures, and filtered drinking water. About 91% of revenue is US-sourced. Sales are specification-driven: engineers and architects spec Zurn products into buildings well before a purchase order is cut, which is what gives the business its pricing power and revenue visibility. Monday's news: a definitive agreement to acquire Intellihot Inc. (private) for roughly $320m cash, entering the tankless/high-efficiency water-heating category for the first time, expected to close in early Q3. CEO Todd Adams simultaneously pre-announced that Q2 results will land above the guidance range the company itself gave earlier in the quarter, with full detail due on the 29 July earnings call. Three KPIs matter most: (1) **organic revenue growth** — Q1 2026 revenue was $433m, +11.4% y/y, with trailing-twelve-month revenue of ~$1.74bn, +10% y/y; (2) **operating margin** — expanded to 19.0% in Q1 from 16.3% a year earlier; (3) **capital-allocation discipline on M&A** — Intellihot adds only about $37m of 2026 net sales against a $320m price tag, roughly 8.6x revenue, a real test of whether this bolt-on is disciplined or a growth-starved reach.

> [!bullish]
> **6.2 Bull:** A high-margin, pure-play water compounder is bolting on an adjacent, regulation-favoured category (energy-efficient water heating) at the same time it's beating its own guidance — organic and inorganic momentum arriving together.

> [!bearish]
> **6.3 Bear:** Paying roughly 8.6x revenue for a company doing only ~$37m in sales is a rich multiple carrying real integration risk, and reaching into adjacencies for growth can be an early signal that the core water-infrastructure market is maturing.

**6.4 Differentiating question:** Does Intellihot's tankless technology actually get cross-sold at scale through Zurn's existing spec-driven relationships with engineers and architects, or does it stay a bolt-on with limited synergy? That distinction is what separates disciplined capital allocation from a growth-starved reach into an adjacent category — the 29 July earnings call's guidance detail and any follow-up M&A commentary is the first real test.

## 7. Trade Floor — Two Open Tensions

**7.1 FICC/oil — Is the Hormuz physical-flow collapse (5.1m bpd, down from 12.5m a week ago) a durable supply shock, or a temporary function of insurers pulling back that normalizes once someone re-underwrites the risk?** One side: actual transit counts near zero, not just costlier insurance, is a genuine realized disruption — the kind of event that historically takes weeks, not days, to reverse even after a ceasefire, because insurers and shippers rebuild confidence slowly. Other side: war-risk premiums are a market mechanism, not a physical barrier — at the right price, or with a government backstop (naval escort, state reinsurance), flows could resume quickly once the immediate strike risk eases. *Settling datapoint: whether daily Hormuz transit counts recover this week, and whether any government steps in with an escort or reinsurance backstop.*

**7.2 Equity — Was Korea's "Black Monday" (-4.46%) a genuine fundamentals repricing of AI-capex risk, or a technical overshoot amplified by sell-sidecar circuit breakers?** One side: Bloomberg's "more about memory than compute" framing, TSMC's much milder reaction, and Moonshot itself hitting a GPU capacity wall within 48 hours all argue the underlying HBM/memory demand case is intact — this was mechanical amplification, not new fundamental information. Other side: Citi downgraded the Kospi outright, and a near-5% single-day move well past Thursday's already-confirmed rout is a lot to wave away as pure mechanics. *Settling datapoint: SK Hynix's 29 July earnings call — does HBM pricing and capacity-commitment language hold or crack?*

## 11. Journal Seed

The sharpest thing to form a view on today is that the Hormuz story just crossed from *priced risk* to *realized supply shock*: confirmed transits have fallen to 5.1m barrels/day from 12.5m a week ago, and insurers are cancelling war-risk cover outright rather than just charging more for it — a genuine physical disruption, not merely a repricing of tail risk. That's landed in the same 24 hours as a UK Chancellor's shock sacking and Korea's own "Black Monday."

> If Hormuz's physical flow has already collapsed by more than half — not just gotten more expensive to insure — is Brent's roughly 15% weekly move actually pricing in a genuine multi-week supply disruption, or is the market still assuming alternative routes and reserve releases will show up fast enough to cap the damage? What would this week's actual OPEC+ output response, or a confirmed Japanese strategic-reserve release, need to look like to settle that for you?
