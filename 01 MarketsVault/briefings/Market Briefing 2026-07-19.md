## 2. Theme Tracker

### 2.1 fed-path — US Fed path & inflation — TURNING (more hawkish)
- **What changed:** The Fed entered its pre-FOMC blackout period Saturday (18–30 July) — no official speakers until the meeting itself. Bloomberg's weekend piece frames an internal "family fight" over how to treat energy- and AI-driven inflation under Chair Warsh, extending Thursday's hawkish testimony rather than reversing it. A week-ahead roundup cited prediction-market pricing showing July hike odds near 46%, up sharply from the ~89–90% hold-odds this desk noted Thursday — a single-source data point worth confirming against CME-style futures pricing once markets reopen, but directionally consistent with the week's tone.
- **Market effect:** Counterintuitively, the 10-year Treasury eased to 4.55% Friday (from ~4.58% Thursday) even as hawkish repricing chatter built — see 2.4 for why. DXY held roughly flat near 100.76.
- **Concept:** Prediction markets and rate-futures prices are a probability-weighted average of possible outcomes, not a forecast of the single most likely one. A jump from ~10% to ~46% hike odds doesn't mean a hike is now expected — it means the market sees a genuine coin-flip where it previously saw a near-certainty, which is itself a large repricing of uncertainty even before the FOMC says a word.

### 2.2 hormuz-oil — Hormuz / Iran war — STRENGTHENING (confirmed strikes, not just threats)
- **What changed:** The conflict crossed from threatened enforcement to confirmed multi-country strikes over the weekend. Iran's Revolutionary Guard claims it disabled two more tankers in the Strait Saturday; a separate Iranian strike on a Kuwaiti vessel wounded four Kuwaiti naval personnel; Iran also struck targets tied to Bahrain, Jordan, Oman, Qatar and Syria in retaliation for US strikes. A mediation track continues (Iran's FM Araghchi met Oman's FM in Muscat Saturday; the US met Lebanon's president Sunday), but senior Israeli officials warned of unconditional retaliation if Iran strikes again.
- **Market effect:** Brent spiked 4.6% Friday to ~$88.10 (WTI +4.5% to $82.49) — a one-month high and +14% on the week, the sharpest weekly oil move of this conflict. Roughly 84% of Hormuz crude/condensate flows go to Asia, with China alone taking about a third — direct exposure for Asian refiners into Monday.
- **Concept:** War-risk insurance premiums are forward-looking; the spot oil price is often stickier, moving only once actual disruption is confirmed rather than merely threatened. This week's jump shows the spot market re-rating to catch up with what underwriters had already priced — the gap between "threat priced" and "confirmed-event priced" closing in a single session.

### 2.3 ai-capex — AI capex cycle — TURNING (bear case gets a new argument)
- **What changed:** Chinese startup Moonshot released "Kimi K3," billed as the world's largest open-source model — a fresh version of January 2025's "DeepSeek moment," where a much cheaper competitor model reopens doubt about whether trillion-dollar Western AI capex buys a durable moat. The reaction was sharp: Nikkei -4.03% Friday to 64,141 (TOPIX -2.72%), now down over 11% from its 25 June record — a confirmed correction — with Tokyo Electron -9%, Advantest -9.4%, SoftBank -9.2%. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index fell into its own bear market. Samsung's preliminary Q2 print added to the pattern: record HBM-driven profit, but a sales miss and a >6% share-price fall anyway (see 1.5). Hang Seng Tech had its worst week in over a year.
- **Market effect:** Nasdaq -1.4% Friday to 25,520; Stoxx 600 -0.34%; the chip-sector selloff was the dominant driver of Friday's US and European closes, running in parallel with (not caused by) the oil spike.
- **Concept:** The market doesn't need proof that a cheaper model actually matches Western frontier capability — the mere plausibility is enough to force a repricing, because it attacks the core assumption behind the AI-capex bull case: that more compute spend equals a durable moat. If a fraction of the spend can get close to the same output, the return on all that committed capex gets questioned immediately, even before anyone independently verifies the claim.

### 2.4 bond-regime — Bond market regime — modestly firmer, and disconnected from the usual oil-shock playbook
- **What changed:** The 10-year Treasury eased to ~4.55% Friday from ~4.58% Thursday, even as Brent spiked 14% on the week — the opposite of the textbook "oil shock raises inflation fear, inflation fear raises yields" pattern.
- **Market effect:** DXY held roughly flat (~100.76). Gold rose about 1% on Friday itself but was still down roughly 3% on the week, touching levels last seen since November — suggesting the weekly picture remains dollar/real-yield-driven even though Friday saw a small safe-haven bid.
- **Concept:** A geopolitical shock can pull bond yields in two directions at once — the inflation-fear channel (oil costs more, prices rise, yields should rise to compensate) and the flight-to-quality channel (investors get scared and buy safe government debt regardless of inflation, pushing yields down). This week, flight-to-quality appears to have temporarily won out over inflation fear. Whether that holds once the shock stops being new information — and inflation data starts actually reflecting the oil move — is the thing to watch into the FOMC.

## 5. Deep Dive — Andy Burnham Becomes UK Prime Minister Monday

**5.1 What happened:** Andy Burnham, the former Greater Manchester mayor, was declared unopposed leader of the UK's governing Labour Party at a special conference Friday, after an internal rebellion forced Keir Starmer's resignation. Starmer remains PM in name only until Monday, when he formally resigns to King Charles III, who then invites Burnham to form a government — Britain's seventh prime minister in a decade, installed via internal party mechanics rather than a general election. ([PBS](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/andy-burnham-is-declared-leader-of-uks-labour-party-pledges-to-restore-hope), [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/17/burnham-confirmed-as-leader-of-uks-governing-labour-party-headed-for-pm), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/07/17/uk/andy-burnham-labour-leader-uk-intl))

**5.2 Why it matters:** Burnham is seen as more left-leaning than Starmer or Chancellor Rachel Reeves, and markets have been pricing execution risk since leadership speculation began in May — 10-year gilt yields spiked to 5.137% and sterling hit a one-month low at the peak of that uncertainty. This is a genuine sovereign-leadership-change event for a G7 economy, distinct from any of the four running themes, with direct fiscal-policy stakes for gilts, sterling and UK equities.

**5.3 What's priced:** Burnham's pre-confirmation policy speech explicitly committed to keeping Reeves's existing fiscal rules, which calmed gilts and lifted sterling — so the "fiscally reckless left populist" tail scenario looks largely priced out already. What's *not* priced: cabinet composition (whether Reeves herself stays as Chancellor), and the concrete shape of the Autumn Budget and any tax package. Bloomberg/Aegon estimate roughly 20bp of residual political-risk premium still sits in 10-year gilts. Burnham has already scrapped the digital ID scheme, an early signal he'll reverse some Starmer-era policy even while keeping the fiscal framework intact. ([Bloomberg/Aegon](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/gilt-market-is-giving-burnham-a-pass-for-now-says-aegon-s-lynch))

**5.4 Monitor:** Monday's formal handover and first cabinet announcements — especially whether Reeves stays as Chancellor — plus any early signal on the Autumn Budget's tax and spending stance once gilts reopen Monday morning London time.

## 6. Idea Lab — [[Saab AB]] (SAAB-B.ST / ADR: SAABF)

*Learning exercise, not a recommendation. Chosen for real news flow (Friday's Q2 beat) and as a deliberate rotation into European defense industrials, away from the past two editions' tech-heavy picks (Netflix, CXMT).*

**6.1 How it makes money:** Saab runs five segments — Aeronautics (Gripen fighter jets), Dynamics (missiles and anti-tank/anti-ship weapons), Surveillance (radar and C4I systems), Kockums (submarines), and Support & Services. Friday's Q2 print: sales SEK 25.5bn (vs. SEK 23.9bn expected), EBIT SEK 2.8bn at an 11% margin — a record — and a record order backlog of SEK 317.7bn, up from SEK 197.6bn a year ago, with order bookings up 141% y/y driven largely by a SEK 47bn Polish submarine order. Three KPIs matter most: (1) **order backlog and book-to-bill** — currently very strong, with roughly 60% of the backlog deliverable within 2.5 years, giving unusually clear multi-year revenue visibility for an industrial; (2) **EBIT margin** — this quarter's 11% is a record, the test being whether it holds as production scales; (3) **free cash flow conversion** — historically Saab's weak point given long-cycle defense contracts, but H1 cash flow flipped to +SEK 1bn from -SEK 1.1bn a year ago. CEO Micael Johansson also called Friday for Europe to overhaul its fragmented national weapons-procurement systems and said Gripen output could rise from 15/year to 25–30/year.

> [!bullish]
> **6.2 Bull:** A record SEK 318bn backlog plus a structural European rearmament cycle — driven by Poland and the broader Ukraine-adjacent buildup — gives Saab multi-year revenue visibility that almost no other industrial sector can match right now.

> [!bearish]
> **6.3 Bear:** The stock has already fallen from a 52-week high of SEK 748.8 to roughly SEK 520 as investors question whether the order book has outrun delivery capacity — doubling Gripen output while holding an 11% margin is unproven execution risk, not a demonstrated capability.

**6.4 Differentiating question:** Can Saab actually convert its order backlog into cash at scale — sustaining or improving both the 11% EBIT margin and the newly positive free-cash-flow trend — while roughly doubling Gripen production, or does scaling defense manufacturing this fast structurally compress margins the way it has for other suppliers mid-rearmament cycle? The next two quarters' free-cash-flow trajectory, not the backlog headline, is what will actually answer that.

## 7. Trade Floor — Two Open Tensions

**7.1 Equity — Is Kimi K3 a real "DeepSeek moment" for AI capex, or noise the market will forget by Monday?** One side: this is real evidence the compute-scarcity thesis is cracking — a Chinese open-source model claiming frontier-adjacent capability, a confirmed Nikkei correction, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index falling into its own bear market are hard to wave away as sentiment alone. Other side: the January 2025 DeepSeek shock didn't actually stop hyperscaler capex growth in the quarters that followed, and this week's own data cuts against the bear case — TSMC's CoWoS packaging capacity is "so tight it's limiting customer growth," and Samsung just posted record HBM-driven operating profit. *Settling datapoint: Samsung's 30 July earnings call and SK Hynix's 29 July call — do HBM pricing and capacity-commitment language hold, or crack?*

**7.2 FICC/oil — Does this weekend's confirmed tanker strikes mean Hormuz transit risk is now structural rather than a tail scenario?** One side: actual strikes on multiple tankers, plus retaliatory hits across five countries in a single weekend, mark a real escalation threshold crossed — Brent's 14% weekly jump is the spot market catching up to what war-risk insurers already knew. Other side: a mediation track is still active (the Oman-brokered talks), OPEC+ retains spare capacity and has kept adding supply, and rhetoric has outpaced confirmed physical disruption to actual transit volumes so far this conflict. *Settling datapoint: confirmed daily Hormuz vessel transit counts and whether insurers push war-risk premiums higher again this week.*
