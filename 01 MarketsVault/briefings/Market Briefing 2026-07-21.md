## 2. Theme Tracker

### 2.1 fed-path — US Fed path & inflation — stable (marginal hawkish drift, no new data)
- **What changed:** Hold-probability for the 29 July FOMC eased to ~83% from ~87% Monday — a modest drift, not a repricing. It's driven entirely by oil/geopolitical spillover into inflation expectations (see 2.2), not by any new economic release. The blackout period (18-30 July) is confirmed holding — Chair Warsh's last public remarks were 15 July, pre-blackout. Next CPI print lands 12 August, well after the FOMC decision.
- **Market effect:** US 10-year Treasury continued to firm, ~4.60-4.64% versus Monday's ~4.55-4.60% close — a two-month high — though this is being driven more by the oil/inflation-risk story than by Fed repricing per se.
- **Concept:** When a rate-odds number moves without any new economic data behind it, the move is telling you something about the *inputs* to the Fed's reaction function (like an oil shock raising expected inflation), not about the Fed's own thinking, which by design you can't observe during a blackout. Separating "the Fed changed its mind" from "the world changed and the Fed will have to react to it eventually" is a useful habit whenever odds move during a blackout window.

### 2.2 hormuz-oil — Hormuz / Iran war — STRENGTHENING further (new fronts opening)
- **What changed:** The US struck Iran for a 10th consecutive night. A tanker was attacked in the Strait of Hormuz early Tuesday, its crew forced to abandon ship, while Iran's IRGC claims to have struck US assets in Bahrain and Kuwait. Most significantly, the Houthis — a separate Iran-aligned group — threatened to blockade Saudi shipping through Bab al-Mandab, the Red Sea chokepoint, putting a further ~2.5m bpd of Saudi exports at risk through an entirely different route than Hormuz. War-risk insurance is holding near ~5% of hull value per voyage (up to $3-10m on a $100m tanker), now described as the "new market norm" rather than a spike.
- **Market effect:** Brent actually eased to ~$88.9 (-0.4%) Tuesday, off Monday's break above $90 (intraday high $91.41) — diplomatic mediation chatter is capping gains even as the physical conflict widens. WTI firmer, ~$83-84 (+2.2%). Gold extended its safe-haven bid to ~$4,054-4,080 (+1.5-2%), up from Monday's ~$4,017.
- **Concept:** A single chokepoint failing is a supply shock; two independent chokepoints failing simultaneously is a much harder problem, because there's no obvious alternate route left to reroute cargo through. The market's current pricing — oil actually *softer* on a day the conflict added a second front — suggests traders are treating the Bab al-Mandab threat as noise or leverage rather than a credible near-term blockade. That's a bet, not a certainty; watch whether it's still holding once a single tanker is actually hit or turned back there.

- **Market effect:** The UK move is idiosyncratic (political relief rally), while the US move reflects the same oil-shock inflation logic running through Theme 2.1. Two different mechanisms producing yield moves in opposite short-term directions on the same day.
- **Concept:** A "calming" political statement can move a bond market even with zero change in the actual fiscal math behind it — traders are pricing the *removal of tail risk* (a chaotic, inexperienced Chancellor) rather than any concrete improvement in the deficit outlook. That's a real but temporary relief, and it's worth distinguishing from a genuine credibility upgrade, which would need to show up in an actual budget, not a first-day speech.

## 5. Deep Dive — [[Intersnack Group]] Takes [[Utz Brands]] Private for $2.9bn

**5.1 What happened:** German snack maker Intersnack Group agreed to acquire all outstanding shares of Utz Brands (NYSE: UTZ) for $14.25 per share in cash — a roughly 91% premium to Monday's close — in a deal valuing the company at approximately $2.9bn. Post-close, Utz becomes privately held, split 50/50 between Intersnack and entities controlled by the founding Rice and Lissette families, who are rolling over their existing equity rather than cashing out. Financing is a mix of roughly $920m of Intersnack cash, a new $1.1bn term loan, and a $250m asset-based lending facility. The deal is expected to close in Q4 2026, at which point Utz delists from the NYSE. Utz shares rose roughly 89-90% intraday Tuesday. ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/21/snacks-maker-utz-brands-to-be-taken-private-in-2point9-billion-deal.html), [Businesswire](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260720176955/en/Utz-Brands-Inc.-and-Intersnack-Group-GmbH-Co.-KG-Announce-Agreement-to-Take-Utz-Private-and-Partnership-with-Founding-Family))

**5.2 Why it matters:** There was no prior takeover speculation on Utz — the stock had simply drifted down about 47% over the past year on soft category growth, which is exactly why an unrumored, 91%-premium bid landed so hard. It's a clean, real-time example of how a strategic acquirer values a business the public market has given up on differently than the market itself does, and of a hybrid deal structure — part strategic buyer, part founding-family rollover — that's less common than a pure private-equity buyout.

**5.3 What's priced:** With the deal announced and terms set, Utz should now trade close to $14.25 minus a small arbitrage discount reflecting the time to close (Q4 2026) and standard regulatory/financing risk — this is a textbook risk-arbitrage spread to watch, not a story still being repriced. What isn't yet priced or disclosed: whether antitrust review of a European strategic buyer absorbing a US snack brand draws any scrutiny, and how the new $1.1bn term loan is priced given current credit-market conditions.

**5.4 Monitor:** Any regulatory filings or antitrust commentary as the deal moves toward Q4 close; whether the arb spread behaves as a clean "deal certainty" trade or widens on financing-market jitters; and whether this premium recalibrates how the market values other beaten-down, private-label-exposed US packaged-food names.

## 6. Idea Lab — [[Halliburton]] (NYSE: HAL)

*Learning exercise, not a recommendation. Rotation into Energy/oilfield services — fresh sector for this week, and a direct single-stock read on the tension between an oil-price spike (Hormuz) and softening North American drilling demand.*

**6.1 How it makes money:** Halliburton runs two segments. Completion & Production (~55% of revenue) covers cementing, stimulation/fracturing and artificial lift — work billed largely on a per-job basis tied to how many wells are being completed. Drilling & Evaluation (~45%) covers drilling fluids, wireline, drill bits and project management, priced more on day-rate/technology-mix. Q2 2026 (reported today): adjusted EPS $0.55 versus $0.54 expected, revenue $5.71bn versus $5.51bn expected — a beat on both lines — yet shares fell over 6% after management warned North American oilfield-services demand is weakening faster than previously anticipated. International revenue was the offset, at $3.4bn (+5% sequentially), led by Latin America (+22% year-on-year in Q1) and Europe/Africa (+11%), while North America continued to soften. Three KPIs matter most: (1) North America land completions pricing and activity — the segment under acute pressure; (2) international rig count and project-management contract wins, especially in Latin America and the Middle East; (3) technology/digital mix — premium directional-drilling and digital services that are propping up margin (~14% operating margin) even as volumes soften. ([Halliburton Q2 2026 release](https://www.halliburton.com/en/about-us/press-release/halliburton-announces-second-quarter-2026-results), [Proactive Investors](https://www.proactiveinvestors.com/companies/news/1095786/halliburton-tops-q2-earnings-estimates-as-ceo-warns-of-softer-oilfield-services-market-1095786.html))

> [!bullish]
> **6.2 Bull:** International growth in Latin America and Europe/Africa, plus a multi-year offshore/national-oil-company upcycle, can offset North American softness and keep margins expanding through a richer technology/digital mix.

> [!bearish]
> **6.3 Bear:** Management's own admission that North American demand is deteriorating faster than expected — arriving in the same week oil is spiking on a war, not falling — suggests E&P capital discipline, not price, is now the binding constraint on drilling activity, and that international growth alone can't offset it.

**6.4 Differentiating question:** Is Halliburton's North American slowdown a customer-mix problem specific to its private E&P client base, or an industry-wide capital-discipline signal that will show up identically at rival SLB's print? The answer determines whether this week's oil-price spike (Hormuz) ever translates into more North American drilling activity, or whether E&Ps have simply structurally decoupled capex decisions from the spot oil price — the OPEC+ meeting on 2 August is the next read on whether that discipline holds industry-wide.

## 11. Journal Seed

The sharpest under-priced risk today is the Houthis' new threat to blockade Saudi shipping through Bab al-Mandab — a second Gulf chokepoint, entirely separate from Hormuz, that would remove much of the rerouting flexibility that's made this month's Hormuz disruption survivable so far. Brent actually eased Tuesday even as the conflict widened to a 10th strike night, a new tanker attack, and claimed strikes on Bahrain and Kuwait — a sign the market may be treating the new threat as leverage rather than a credible near-term blockade.

If both Hormuz and Bab al-Mandab were genuinely constrained at once, what redundancy is actually left in Gulf export routes — and is Brent's current ~$89 level anywhere close to pricing that scenario, or still implicitly assuming only one chokepoint fails at a time? What would an actual vessel incident at Bab al-Mandab this week need to look like to make you update?
