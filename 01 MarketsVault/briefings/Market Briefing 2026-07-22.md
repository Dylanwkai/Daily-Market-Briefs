## 5. Deep Dive — Federal Judge Freezes $110bn [[Paramount Skydance]]–[[Warner Bros. Discovery]] Merger

**5.1 What happened:** On 20 July, US District Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín (N.D. Cal.) granted a 14-day temporary restraining order blocking Paramount Skydance from closing its roughly $110bn all-cash acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery — a deal that had been scheduled to close today, 22 July. Twelve states led by California AG Rob Bonta sued last week, arguing the combination would merge two of the top three US cable programmers and two of the top five film distributors under David Ellison's control, giving the combined company an estimated 27% share of domestic theatrical distribution. A preliminary-injunction hearing is now set for 3 August. WBD shares fell roughly 3.8-4% to $25.86 on the ruling — about 20% below Paramount's original $31/share cash offer — while Paramount Skydance shares hit a fresh 52-week low near $8.56-8.70. ([Fool.com](https://www.fool.com/coverage/stock-market-today/2026/07/20/stock-market-today-july-20-warner-bros-discovery-falls-4-as-judge-pauses-usd110-billion-paramount-skydance-deal/), [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/07/20/judge-halts-paramount-warner-81-billion-merger/), [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/media/judge-puts-temporary-pause-paramount-warner-bros-merger-rcna587804))

**5.2 Why it matters:** This is the first serious test of a red-state/blue-state coalition using state antitrust law — not the DOJ or FTC — to block a federally-cleared mega-merger. If the states prevail even temporarily, it's a template other state AGs could reach for on future media or tech deals, effectively adding a second, less predictable layer of merger review beneath the federal one.

**5.3 What's priced in:** The WBD/Paramount Skydance arbitrage spread has "ballooned" past historical deal-certainty norms, according to Bloomberg, implying the market is now pricing meaningful odds of delay or collapse rather than a clean 3 August win for Ellison. Paramount is on the hook for a $7bn breakup fee if regulatory failure kills the deal, plus a $0.25/share/day "ticking fee" to WBD holders for any delay past 30 September (roughly $7m/day) — a cost the market is starting to fold into WBD's price. What isn't yet priced: whether Paramount offers a divestiture package to placate the states, or whether more states or the FTC itself join in. ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/paramount-warner-deal-psky-wbd-spread-balloons-as-ruling-upends-timing), [Star-Advertiser](https://www.staradvertiser.com/2026/07/20/breaking-news/judge-pauses-paramounts-110-billion-warner-bros-merger/))

**5.4 Monitor:** The 3 August preliminary-injunction hearing; any asset-divestiture concessions floated by Paramount; whether additional states or federal regulators intervene; and whether the WBD/Paramount Skydance spread continues widening (pricing collapse) or begins tightening (pricing confidence in an eventual close).

## 6. Idea Lab — [[Texas Instruments]] (Nasdaq: TXN)

*Learning exercise, not a recommendation. Rotation into Industrials/Analog Semiconductors — a deliberately different chip story from the HBM/AI-accelerator names dominating the Theme Tracker, and directly tied to today's earnings.*

**6.1 How it makes money:** TXN runs two segments. Analog (~75% of revenue) makes power-management and signal-chain chips — the unglamorous components that manage voltage, sense signals and control motors in everything from cars to factory equipment — typically low-dollar, high-volume, and priced on decades-long product life cycles rather than leading-edge speed. Embedded Processing (~15-20%) covers microcontrollers for embedded systems. Unlike Nvidia or TSMC, TXN owns roughly 80%+ of its own manufacturing on mature-node 300mm wafers — larger wafers than most peers use even for legacy analog chips — a structural cost advantage built over a decade of in-house fab investment. Q2 2026 (reported today): EPS $2.14 versus $1.95 expected (+23.5% surprise), revenue $5.46bn versus $5.24bn expected (+23% year-on-year), with broad-based growth across industrial, data-center and automotive end markets. Three KPIs matter most: (1) inventory days and the book-to-bill ratio — the cleanest read on whether this beat is a genuine industrial recovery or a one-quarter restocking bounce after a long destocking cycle; (2) automotive/industrial mix versus personal electronics — the former is stickier and higher-margin; (3) capex-to-revenue on the 300mm fab buildout — TXN has run capex well above depreciation for years betting on future demand, and this quarter is the first real evidence that bet might be paying off. ([SEC 8-K](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000097476/000009747626000148/q22026txnex99-eredgar.htm))

> [!bullish]
> **6.2 Bull:** A +23% year-on-year, broad-based beat across industrial, data-center and automotive — after a multi-year destocking cycle — signals genuine end-demand recovery, not just easy comps, and TXN's in-house 300mm cost advantage should let margins expand disproportionately as volumes return.

> [!bearish]
> **6.3 Bear:** One strong quarter after a prolonged inventory drawdown looks a lot like restocking, not a structural demand step-up — distributors and OEMs rebuilding safety stock can produce exactly this kind of beat without any change in underlying end-market consumption, and it can reverse just as fast once channels refill.

**6.4 Differentiating question:** Does TXN's Q3 guidance and book-to-bill ratio show inventory normalizing toward a steady state, or does the beat unwind as quickly as it appeared? The read-through matters beyond TXN itself — as the most diversified analog/industrial bellwether reporting this early in the cycle, its answer previews whether the broader industrial economy (not just AI-linked chip demand) is actually reaccelerating, or whether today's number was restocking dressed up as a recovery.

## 11. Journal Seed

Alphabet beat on every headline number today — 82% cloud growth, a revenue beat, margins intact — and the stock still fell, purely because 2026 capex guidance held flat instead of rising and 2027 capex was flagged to go "significantly" higher. That's a genuinely new signal in the AI-capex theme: the market may be starting to treat hyperscaler capex like any other capital-intensive investment, judged on free-cash-flow payback, rather than rewarding growth-story momentum unconditionally.

Is this the start of the market demanding ROI discipline from every hyperscaler's capex guide, or just one skittish session reacting to one number? Microsoft, Meta and Amazon's own capex guides in the coming weeks will tell you which — watch whether their stocks get punished on flat-to-rising capex too, or whether Alphabet's reaction was idiosyncratic.
